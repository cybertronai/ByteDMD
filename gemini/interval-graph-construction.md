The connection between an optimal spatial memory allocator and the **Minimum Chromatic Sum (MCS)** of an Interval Graph is one of the most elegant reductions in theoretical computer science. It perfectly bridges the physical constraints of Bill Dally's 2D silicon layout with pure graph theory.

By translating your program's execution trace into a graph, we can mathematically prove exactly how an omniscient compiler bypasses the Bélády (OPT) sorting constraints to achieve an even lower energy cost.

Here is the formal geometric reduction, followed by a walkthrough of how Graph Coloring finds the spatial loophole that Bélády misses.

### ---

**The Reduction: Physics to Graph Theory**

We want to find the absolute minimum energy required to execute an algorithm using an **Optimal Static Spatial Allocator**.

*Static Allocation* (in this context) means that when a variable is written, it is assigned a specific physical slot (distance) on the chip, and it must wait in that slot until it is read by the ALU.

Here is how this physical reality maps 1:1 onto the **Weighted Minimum Chromatic Sum** problem:

#### **1\. Vertices \= Lifespan Intervals**

Every time a variable is written, it begins a dormant "wait time" that ends the exact moment it is read by the ALU. (If a variable is read multiple times and writes are free, the compiler can relocate it, so each read-to-read gap is its own independent interval).

* We map every single dormant lifespan as a **Vertex ($v$)**.  
* This vertex represents a strict temporal **Interval** $\[t_{start}, t_{read}\]$.

#### **2\. Edges \= Time Overlaps (Spatial Exclusivity)**

In Dally’s physical 2D grid, two variables cannot occupy the exact same physical slot at the exact same time.

* If Interval $A$ and Interval $B$ overlap in time (even for a single clock cycle), they are in spatial conflict. We draw an **Edge** between them.  
* Because the graph is constructed entirely from overlapping line segments on a 1D timeline, it creates an **Interval Graph**, a famously structured class of *Perfect Graphs*.

#### **3\. Colors \= Physical Distance**

In graph coloring, connected vertices must be assigned different colors.

* We define the "Colors" as the integer depths on the geometric stack ($1, 2, 3 \dots$).  
* Coloring the graph is mathematically identical to ensuring no two overlapping variables are assigned to the same physical slot.

#### **4\. The Objective \= Minimizing Geometric Fetch Cost**

Standard compiler register allocation tries to minimize the *number* of colors used (the Chromatic Number). This minimizes peak memory capacity. But in Bill Dally's model, we don't care how many outer slots we use; we care about the *energy* of the slots we use.

* Reading a variable assigned to Color $c$ (Depth $c$) costs energy $C(c) = \lceil\sqrt{c}\rceil$.  
* Therefore, the spatial compiler’s goal is to find a valid coloring that minimizes the sum of all vertex fetch costs: $\sum_{v \in V} \lceil\sqrt{c(v)}\rceil$.  
* This is the exact definition of the **Generalized Weighted Minimum Chromatic Sum** problem.

### ---

**How Chromatic Sum Defeats the Bélády Stack**

To see the absolute brilliance of this graph reduction, let’s revisit the 4-variable trace from earlier where the manual allocator beat the Bélády max\_rank bound.

**The Trace Timeline:**

* $t=0$: Write A *(read at t=6)* $\rightarrow$ **Interval A: \[0, 6\]**  
* $t=1$: Write B *(read at t=3)* $\rightarrow$ **Interval B: \[1, 3\]**  
* $t=2$: Write C *(read at t=5)* $\rightarrow$ **Interval C: \[2, 5\]**  
* $t=4$: Write D *(read at t=7)* $\rightarrow$ **Interval D: \[4, 7\]**

#### **The Bélády Trap (Strict Sorting)**

Bélády forces data to act like a stack. At $t=2$, B, C, and A are alive. Because A is needed last, it is pushed to Rank 3\.

At $t=4$, D is born. The stack looks at D's deadline ($t=7$), sees that it is later than C($5$) and A($6$), and forces D to the bottom of the stack at Rank 3\.

* Bélády assumes D *must* push from the top down. It charges A and D both for Distance 3\. **Total energy \= 7\.**

#### **The Graph Theory Solution (Spatial Bypassing)**

Now look at the graph generated by our intervals.

* A spans the whole time, so it connects to B, C, and D.  
* C connects to B and D.  
* **Crucial Insight: B \[1, 3\] and D \[4, 7\] DO NOT OVERLAP.** There is no edge between them\!

The Minimum Chromatic Sum algorithm sees that B and D are not connected. Therefore, **they can share the exact same Color\!**

**The Optimal Coloring:**

1. Color B \= 1 (Cost \= 1\)  
2. Color D \= 1 (Cost \= 1\) $\leftarrow$ *D recycles B's slot perfectly\!*  
3. Color C \= 2 (Cost \= $\lceil\sqrt{2}\rceil = 2$)  
4. Color A \= 3 (Cost \= $\lceil\sqrt{3}\rceil = 2$)

**Total Chromatic Sum \= 1 \+ 1 \+ 2 \+ 2 \= 6\.**

### ---

**The Profound Theoretical Takeaways**

By mapping your execution to an Interval Graph, you mathematically formalize the concept of **Spatial Bypassing**.

The Chromatic Sum optimizer doesn't care about the Bélády "next-use" sorting order. It realizes a profound loophole: **Sometimes it is cheaper to intentionally paint a vertex with a BAD color (a high depth) to leave a GOOD color (a low depth) open to be recycled by a swarm of smaller intervals.** It intentionally leaves older data (C and A) floating in the outer orbits, because paying to shuffle them inward would destroy the overall energy savings.

**The "Mic Drop" Consequence: NP-Hardness**

While finding the standard Chromatic Number (footprint) of an interval graph is easy ($O(N \log N)$), Kenneth Supowit proved in 1987 (and Dániel Marx expanded in 2005\) that computing the **Minimum Chromatic Sum of an Interval Graph is strongly NP-Complete**.

This proves that **no perfect polynomial-time algorithm can ever exist to statically allocate Bill Dally's 2D grid.**

This is exactly why your proxy metrics—like the continuous Bélády max\_rank or the Sleator-Tarjan capacity envelopes—are so vital. They give you fast, mathematically rigorous $O(N \log N)$ bounds that tightly shadow the true physics of the silicon, without forcing you to solve an impossible, NP-Hard combinatorial packing problem.
